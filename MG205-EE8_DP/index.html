Research question

Main question

  1. Does the death penalty deter homicide?

Why do we care?

Deterrence hypothesis

  • Economic theory of crime (Becker, 1968).
  • Rational criminals respond to incentives.
  • Higher expected punishment reduces crime.
  • Death penalty is maximum possible sanction.

Brutalization hypothesis

  • State violence legitimizes killing.
  • Devalues human life in society.
  • May increase aggression and homicide.

Potential identification strategy

Judicial experiment

Supreme Court interventions created quasi-experimental conditions:

  • Furman v. Georgia (1972):
    • Invalidated all death penalty statutes.
  • Gregg v. Georgia (1976):
    • Allowed states to reinstate with new procedures.
  • States reinstated at different times (1976-1995).
  • Execution rates varied dramatically across states.

Potential endogeneity sources

  1. Reverse causality: States adopt death penalty in response to crime.
  2. Policy endogeneity: Criminal justice system changes simultaneously.
  3. Omitted variables: Criminal justice approach, societal attitudes.
  4. Measurement: Death penalty laws vs.Â actual executions.

Fixed effects alone are insufficient if these factors vary over time.
