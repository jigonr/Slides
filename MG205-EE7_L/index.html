Blanes i Vidal et al. (2012): Revolving Door Lobbyists

The lobbying industry

What is lobbying?

  • Professional paid advocacy to influence government decisions.
  • Large industry - billions spent yearly in the US.
  • Very lucrative - professionals well compensated.
  • Ethically controversial - money potentially buying influence.

What is a revolving door lobbyist?

  • Former government employees who become lobbyists.
  • 41.6% of all lobbyists are ârevolving doorâ.
  • They generate 56% of all industry revenue.

Research question

Main questions

  1. Why do revolving door lobbyists earn more?
  2. What explains their higher revenues?
  3. How much of their value comes from political connections?

Why do we care?

Connections-driven industry

  • âWashington is all about who you knowâ:
    • Potentially inefficient and unfair.
    • May generate distorted incentives.
    • Could require regulation.

Expertise-driven industry

  • âWhat you know matters mostâ:
    • Possibly efficient allocation of talent.
    • Rewards specialized knowledge.
    • Less need for intervention.

Identification challenge

The key empirical problem

  • Expertise, ability, and connections are confounded.
  • Lobbyists with connections may also have valuable expertise.
  • Simple comparison of connected vs.Â non-connected lobbyists is misleading.

\[\text{Revenue}_{i} = \beta_{0} + \beta_{1} \text{Connections}_{i} + \beta_{2} \text{Expertise}_{i} + \beta_{3} \text{Ability}_{i} + \varepsilon_{i}\]

  • Omitted variable bias if we donât observe all factors.

Empirical strategy

Using politician exits for identification

  • Study what happens when a connected politician leaves office.
  • When a senator or representative exits Congress:
    • Lobbyistâs connection value decreases.
    • Lobbyistâs expertise and ability remain constant.
  • Comparing revenue before and after exit isolates connection value.

\[\text{Revenue}_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta \cdot \text{Political Connections}_{it} + X_{it}' \cdot \theta + \gamma_t^{pc} + \varepsilon_{it}\]

Data and sample

Two main databases

  1. Lobbying Disclosure Act data:
    • Lobbyist-level panel data (1998-2008).
    • Revenue information, clients served.
    • Issues lobbied, Congress/agencies contacted.
  2. Political employment histories:
    • Columbia Booksâ Lobbyist.info.
    • Congressional Staffer Salaries database.
    • Links lobbyists to former employers in government.

Measuring lobbyist revenue

Two revenue measures

  1. Unweighted revenue:
    • Sum of all contract values a lobbyist is involved.
    • Captures âpractice valueâ of the lobbyist.
    • Example: $40,000 contract with 4 lobbyists â each gets $40,000 credit.
  2. Weighted revenue:
    • Contract value divided by team size, then summed.
    • Captures per-worker revenue.
    • Example: $40,000 contract with 4 lobbyists â each gets $10,000 credit.

Descriptive statistics

Revolving door premium

  • Average annual weighted revenue:
    • Ex-congressional staffers: $349,700.
    • Non-revolving door lobbyists: $170,000.
    • 105% premium for connected ex-staffers.
  • Revolving door lobbyists generate 56% of total industry revenue.
    • 34% from ex-congressional staffers alone.

Descriptive statistics

Different types of revolving door lobbyists

  • Ex-congressional staffers: 22.6% of all lobbyists.
    • Personal staffers to politicians.
    • Committee staffers.
  • Ex-congressmen: 2.9% of all lobbyists.
  • Outside Congress: 16.6% of all lobbyists.
    • Executive agencies.
    • White House.
    • Other federal posts.

Panel data model

Core specification

\[\text{Revenue}_{it} = \alpha_{i} + \beta \cdot \text{Political Connections}_{it} + X_{it}' \cdot \theta + \gamma_{t}^{pc} + \varepsilon_{it}\]

where

  • \(\alpha_{i}\): Individual lobbyist fixed effects.
  • \(\text{Political Connections}_{it}\): Number of connected politicians still in office.
  • \(X_{it}\): Lobbyist experience (control).
  • \(\gamma_{t}^{pc}\): Party-chamber-time fixed effects.
  • \(\varepsilon_{it}\): Error term.

Identification assumptions

Difference-in-differences

  1. Exit of politicians is exogenous to lobbying revenue.
  2. No anticipation effects before exit.
  3. No pre-existing trends in revenue.
  4. Revenue of lobbyists suffering a loss of a connection would have evolved similarly to the revenue of lobbyists connected to nonexiting politicians in the same party and chamber combination.
  • Exits are plausibly exogenous because:
    • 50% due to retirement.
    • Many due to electoral defeats.
    • Some due to death, scandal, etc.

Main results

Effects of connections on revenue

  • Senate connection: 24% drop in revenue when senator exits.
  • House connection: 10% drop in revenue when representative exits.

Interpretation

  • Evaluated at median revenue ($760,000/year):
    • Senate connection worth approximately $182,000/year.
    • Both statistically and economically significant.
    • Senate connections more valuable than House connections.

Main results

Event-study design

Key findings

  • No pre-existing trends before exit.
  • No anticipation effects.
  • Sharp and immediate drop after exit.
  • Persistent effect (20% drop even 3 years later).

Main results

Not all connections are equal

  • Senate Finance Committee: 36% revenue drop when senator exits.
  • Senate Appropriations: 45% revenue drop when senator exits.
  • House Ways & Means: 35% revenue drop when representative exits.
  • Other committees: No significant effect when politician exits.

Interpretation

  • Connections to powerful committee members are especially valuable.
  • Budget and finance committees generate the highest premiums.

Main results

Industry Participation Effects

  • Being connected to a serving senator increases probability of working as a lobbyist by 27%
  • Representative connections show smaller, insignificant effects.
  • Connection loss may cause some lobbyists to exit the industry entirely.

Implication

  • Revenue estimates may understate the full value of connections.
  • Hardest-hit lobbyists may leave the industry.

Mechanisms

How do connections create value?

  1. Access to politicians: Direct communication channels.
  2. Network advantages: Wider connections to staff and allies.
  3. Inside information: Knowledge of priorities and openness.
  4. Signaling: Credibility with clients.

Not simply about expertise

  • Expertise is constant but revenue changes.
  • Connection loss has immediate effect on value.

Policy Implications

Regulatory considerations

  1. Cooling-off periods:
    • Restrictions on lobbying immediately after leaving government.
    • Could reduce revolving door incentives.
    • Evidence suggests they would be effective.
  2. Transparency requirements:
    • Disclosure of former government positions.
    • Enables monitoring of potential influence.
  3. Staffing incentives:
    • Higher congressional staff salaries to reduce turnover.
    • Reduced incentives to âcash inâ on connections.

Summary

  • More powerful politicians generate larger premiums.
  • Effects are immediate and persistent.

  • Connections are valuable, and perishable assets.
